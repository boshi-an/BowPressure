1 · Study at a Glance

This study investigates how skilled violinists coordinate body and bow movement, bow–string contact force, and sound production. The goal is to build a research dataset of synchronised motion, force, and audio recordings from advanced violin performance. The collected data will later inform the design and control of a robotic violinist.

Skilled musicians are invited to attend the lab to play the violin while being recorded with a capturing system.

3 · Who Can Participate?

✓ You may participate if you …
  • Are 18 years or older
  • Have advanced violin playing skills (conservatory / university level)
  • Can understand study information in English
  • Are willing to be recorded (video & audio)
✗ Please do not participate if you …
  • Currently have musculoskeletal pain or injury that makes playing uncomfortable or unsafe
  • Have photosensitive epilepsy or a related condition
  • Do not consent to audio or video recording
Health warning — bright lights
The motion capture system uses bright infrared strobe lights. These may cause temporary eye discomfort, glare sensitivity, or visual fatigue. Flickering lights may trigger headaches in susceptible individuals. In rare cases (typically 3–60 Hz), they may provoke photosensitive epileptic seizures. If you have photosensitive epilepsy or a related condition you must not participate.

Additional possible discomforts are temporary muscular fatigue or mild strain from normal playing, and mild inconvenience from the instrumented bow setup. Participants may pause or stop at any time.

4 · What Am I Supposed to Do?

You will attend one in-person session at ETH Zurich. Please prepare one or more musical pieces with a total duration of 1 to 10 minutes that you can play smoothly. The lab provides the violin and bow, and stand — you do not need to bring your own instrument.

Piece selection criteria:

  1. A piece that you can play very well; a music stand will be provided for you to keep sheet music.
  2. A piece that doesn't have copyright issues (for example: avoid commercial/Disney repertoire).
  3. Prefer a full piece (e.g., Bach minuet); selected parts are also welcome (e.g., coda of Sibelius Concerto).
  4. Length should ideally be between 1 to 10 minutes.
  5. Diverse genres are welcomed (folk music, jazz, etc.).

The composition of the session:

  1. 1
    ~15 min Welcome, study overview, and written informed consent
  2. 2
    ~10 min Sensor setup: markers attached to bow and instrument; calibration
  3. 3
    ~10 min Warm-up and practice
  4. 4
    ~20 min Recording — play the prepared piece(s); trials may be repeated to improve quality
  5. 5
    ~10 min Equipment removal, short background form, and wrap-up
You may take breaks at any time, skip any task, or withdraw from the study at any time without giving a reason and without any disadvantage.

5 · Compensation & Souvenir

Participants receive CHF 25 per session (cash or equivalent voucher). A souvenir will also be provided: a 4D reconstructed volumetric mesh file and a 3D printed avatar of the musician's actual playing (You will not get elsewhere!).

6 · Data Protection, Insurance & Ethics Review

Data are collected in coded form. Each participant is assigned a unique code; names and contact details are stored separately from the research dataset on secure ETH Zurich infrastructure, accessible only to the named research team.

The research dataset consists of: motion capture data, force measurements, audio recordings, and limited background information (age category, violin experience). Results may be published in a master's thesis, scientific papers, or academic talks. No conclusions about individual participants will appear in published outputs.

Collected data may be published in anonymised form in a scientific data repository. You may explicitly opt out of non-anonymous publication or sharing with external academic collaborators — this is handled in the consent form.

Damage related to the study is covered by ETH Zurich's public liability insurance (Basler Versicherungen, policy no. 30/4.078.362). This study will only begin after review and approval by the ETH Zurich Ethics Commission.
